The Atlanta Tech Hub emerged from a provocation: what if an office building could feel more like a neighborhood than a corporate campus? Located on a formerly industrial block along the BeltLine, the project had an obligation to contribute to the public life of the street, not retreat from it.

## Open Ground Plane

The building's ground floor is entirely open to the public — a covered market hall that hosts local vendors during the week and a farmers' market on weekends. This was a deliberate move to avoid the dead frontage that plagues most commercial buildings. By giving the ground plane to the community, we created a reason for people to come to the building even if they never set foot in an office.

## Workplace Design

The upper floors are organized around a five-story central atrium that brings daylight deep into the floor plate. Each floor features a mix of enclosed offices, open collaboration zones, and "porches" — deep balconies with operable glass walls that allow tenants to work outdoors year-round in Atlanta's temperate climate. The concrete structure is left exposed throughout, with mechanical systems running in accessible floor trenches rather than above dropped ceilings.

## Material Strategy

The facade is composed of hand-laid brick — a nod to the warehouse vernacular of the surrounding neighborhood — with deep-set punched windows that provide solar shading on the south and west exposures. The courtyard is planted with native hardwoods that will mature over the next two decades to create a genuine urban canopy.

## Performance

The building achieves a LEED Gold rating with an Energy Use Intensity 40% below the Atlanta commercial average. Tenant satisfaction surveys conducted six months after occupancy showed a 92% approval rating for indoor environmental quality, and the ground-floor market is now one of the most visited public spaces on the Westside.
